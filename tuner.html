Methods

We use the YIN algorithm to find the pitch of input sound. The following section describes the Yin method treatment of sound, in a way to directly reflect our situation. Some parts not explicitly spelled out in the paper are elaborated on. We will describe and implement the methods from

A. de Cheveigné and H. Kawahara, “YIN, a fundamental frequency estimator for speech and music,” The Journal of the Acoustical Society of America, vol. 111, no. 4, pp. 1917-1930, Apr. 2002, doi: https://doi.org/10.1121/1.1458024.

The input signal, obtained from the Web Audio API, comes in as a discrete time-series array of amplitudes.

Step 1: Autocorrelation

We use the following autocorrelation function to quantify the similarity between a signal and itself delayed by some lag \(\tau\). $$ r_{t} ( \tau ) = \sum_{j = t + 1}^{t + W - \tau} x_{j} x_{j + \tau}$$ Here, \(r_t(\tau)\) is the autocorrelation function at the time intex \(t\), with a lag of \(\tau\). \(W\) is the integration window size, or the time frame in consideration. We subtract \(\tau\) from the upper summation bound to lessen the effect of larger lag sizes, with the assumption that the fundemental frequency is closer to the origin point \(t\).

The Weiner-Khinchin theorem (in the discrete time case) allows us to compute the autocorrelation function using a Fourier Transform.
To get to this new and convenient definition of the autocorrelation function, we can start by taking the Fourier transform \(\mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi)\) of \(r(\tau)\). We will use the continuous forms of functions to show these conversions.
A continuous version of our autocorrelation is $$ r(\tau) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)x(t + \tau)dt $$ and its Fourier transform is $$ \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}r(\tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau $$ Let's substitue in \(r(\tau)\) and swap the order of integration by Fubini's theorem. $$ \begin{align*} \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)x(t + \tau)dt\right]e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)x(t + \tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau dt \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x(t)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t + \tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau dt \end{align*} $$ If we define and substitue in \(u=t+\tau\), we can see that the inner integral can be represented as a Fourier transform of \(x\) multiplied by an exponent. $$ \begin{align*} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t + \tau)e^{-i2\pi\xi\tau}d\tau &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(u)e^{-i2\pi\xi(u-t)}du \\ &= e^{i2\pi\xi t}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(u)e^{-i2\pi\xi u}du \\ &= e^{i2\pi\xi t}\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \end{align*} $$ Subsituting this back in, we get $$ \begin{align*} \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)e^{i2\pi\xi t}\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)dt \\ &= \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)e^{i2\pi\xi t}dt \end{align*} $$ We may notice that $$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x(t)e^{i2\pi\xi t}dt = \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) $$ giving us $$ \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) = \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) $$ And because for real \(x\), the Fourier transform at a negated frequency is the complex conjugate of the Fourier transform at the non-negated frequency, we get $$ \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) = \overline{\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)} $$ Therefore, $$ \begin{align*} \mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi) &= \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \mathcal{F}\{x\}(-\xi) \\ &= \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) \overline{\mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi)} \\ &= | \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) |^2 \end{align*} $$ Using the inverse Fourier transform, we can get the autocorrelation back out, this time with a formulation faster to compute. $$ \begin{align*} r(\tau) &= \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{r(\tau)\}(\mathcal{F}\{r(\tau)\}(\xi)) \\ &= \mathcal{F}^{-1}\{r(\tau)\}(| \mathcal{F}\{x\}(\xi) |^2) \end{align*} $$ We can compute this with the discrete fourier transform $$ \mathcal{F}\{x\}_k = \sum_{j=t+1}^{t+W}x_j \cdot e^{-i2\pi\frac{k}{W}j} $$ and the inverse discrete fourier transform $$ x_j = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{W-1}\mathcal{F}\{x\}_k \cdot e^{i2\pi\frac{k}{N}j} $$
Step 1: Autocorrelation Function - temporal similarity of the signal to itself


Step 2: Difference Function

Consider this difference function, used to find an unknown period: $$ d_t(\tau) = \sum_{j=1}^{W}(x_j - x_{j-\tau})^2 $$ When \(d(\tau)=0\), we have a tau which is a multiple of the period.

This can be expanded, $$ d_t(\tau) = \sum_{j=1}^{W}(x_j^2 + x_{j-\tau}^2 -2x_jx_{j-\tau})^2 $$ and we can substitute in the autocorrelation function: $$ d_t(\tau) = r_t(0) + r_{t+\tau}(0) - 2r_t(\tau) $$ This is great! We can find the period, and from the period find the frequency.
Step 2: Difference Function - dips show candidates for the period


Step 3: Cumulative Mean Normalized Difference Function

However, there are two issues.
  • When the lag (\(\tau\)) is zero, this function will also be zero.
  • Period signals with a harmonic strucutre causing multiple dips in the difference function could create dips that are not indicative of the period directly.
    • For example, a string playing 440 Hz (A4 in 12 tone equal temperament tuning) may produce a frequency at 440 Hz as well as the second harmonic, 880 Hz. The period of the 440 Hz frequency is \(T_0 = \frac{1}{440}\), and the period at 880 Hz is \(\frac{1}{880} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{440} = \frac{1}{2}T_0\). We will see dips at the period of our "main" tone, as well as half that for the second harmonic.

To address this, consider the "cumulative mean normalized difference function": $$ d_t'(\tau) = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if } \tau = 0 \\ d_t(\tau) / \left[(1/\tau)\sum_{j=1}^{\tau}d_t(j)\right] & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$ We directly address the \(\tau=0\) case with an exception, and divide each difference by the mean difference for \(\tau\) from 0 to the current \(\tau\). This division normalizes each calculated difference to the mean difference accumulated so far, they are only less than 1 when they are less than the running average.

Step 3: Cumulative Mean Normalized Difference Function


Step 4: Absolute Threshold

Another issue which could cause inacuraccies is subharmonic errors. They occur at lower freqencies than the fundemental frequency of the sound, and could have a dip which does not indicate the period of our fundemental frequency.

To address this, we will take the smallest \(\tau\) where \(d_t'(\tau) \lt \text{THRESHOLD}\) for some THRESHOLD such as 0.1

Step 5: Parabolic Interpolation

For each local minima \(\tau\), we will fit a parabola onto \(d_t(\tau-1), d_t(\tau), d_t(\tau+1)\) using the Lagrange Interpolation Formula $$ P(x) = d_t(\tau-1) \frac{(x - \tau)(x - (\tau+1))}{((\tau-1) - \tau)((\tau-1) - (\tau+1))} + d_t(\tau) \frac{(x - (\tau-1))(x - (\tau+1))}{(\tau - (\tau-1))(\tau - (\tau+1))} + d_t(\tau+1) \frac{(x - (\tau-1))(x - \tau)}{((\tau+1) - (\tau-1))((\tau+1) - \tau)} $$
Then, we can take the minimum of this parabola to get a period estimate \(\hat{T}\),
and by taking the reciprocal, a frequency estimate: \(\hat{\xi} = \frac{1}{\hat{T}}\).
Step 5: Parabolic Interpolation for sub-sample precision


Step 6: Best Local Estimate

To take into account how our window being considered could start at different parts of the period, causing differing results, we may take an initial estimate, then repeat our entire estimation process in a local window around that initial estimate.

For each time index \(t\), consider the time frame \(\theta \in [t-T_{\text{max}/2}, t+T_{\text{max}/2}]\).
Find the minimum of \(d_{\theta}'(T_{\theta}) \) and take \(T_{\theta}\) as an initial period estimate for frame \(t\).
Then, re-estimate the frequency using the previous steps, this time only considering the range \([T_{\theta} - .2\cdot T_{\theta}, T_{\theta} + .2\cdot T_{\theta}]\)
